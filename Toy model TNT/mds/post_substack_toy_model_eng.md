# The Productivity Mirage and Technological Advantage

*Through the Balassa-Samuelson Effect, this model reveals that America's technological advantage is substantially offset by the efficiency in the provision of European services.*

In recent years, the international economic debate has been dominated by a recurring narrative which suggests that Europe is falling behind the United States. Growth figures in the tech sector, dominance in artificial intelligence development, and the high market capitalization of major US companies paint a picture of economic divergence. When measuring the size of economies in current dollars and observing the high wages of the North American tech sector, the competitiveness differential appears to widen significantly.

However, one might ask whether this perspective, while true in its technological component, omits a more complex reality regarding the true well-being of citizens. To address this question and isolate the fundamental mechanisms driving this divergence, we have developed a simplified economic model ("Toy Model").

### The economic model of two countries and two sectors

Let us consider a world composed of two major economies that we will define as the "Dynamic" country (representing the United States) and the "Social" country (representative of continental Europe). Both regions produce and consume two fundamental categories of goods.

Firstly, **Tradable Goods** encompass those products traded internationally and subject to global competition, as is the case with software, electronics, or advanced machinery.

Secondly, **Non-Tradable Goods and Services** represent activities consumed locally that do not face direct foreign competition, among which healthcare, education, or personal care stand out.

The model assumes a fundamental starting divergence by positing that the Dynamic country experiences accelerated productivity growth in its technological sector and innovates at a pace the Social country cannot match.

Nevertheless, the Social country maintains a clear structural advantage in the provision of local services. Its welfare, education, and healthcare systems operate with greater aggregate efficiency compared to the Dynamic country's model. This variable is critical under the consideration that services can account for up to 70% of the consumption basket in mature economies.

### The underlying mechanism of the Balassa-Samuelson Effect

As the Dynamic country experiences its technological expansion, companies in that sector increase their profit margins. To lead in attracting talent, they substantially raise their economic incentives, causing the tradable sector to become highly lucrative.

Here, a structural phenomenon known in economic literature as the Balassa-Samuelson Effect comes into play. Professionals in non-tradable sectors, from teachers and healthcare personnel to hospitality workers, live in the Dynamic country but have not experienced productivity increases equivalent to those of software engineers. Despite this, to retain these workers against the aggressive wage competition from the tech sector, the broader economy must generally raise the remuneration across all sectors.

The direct consequence of this generalized wage adjustment is a significant increase in the cost of services. To afford higher wages in a sector that is no more productive than before, and which structurally carries greater inefficiencies than in the Social country, the Dynamic country raises the final prices of its non-tradable goods. In practice, this implies that access to healthcare, education, or basic services becomes steadily and notably more expensive.

### The real impact on citizen well-being

If we look exclusively at traditional macroeconomic indicators expressed in nominal terms, the Dynamic country demonstrates superior performance. Its citizens earn higher nominal incomes, and its Gross Domestic Product (GDP) grows at a faster rate.

Yet, the perspective transforms drastically when adjusting these macroeconomic data for the real cost of living using Purchasing Power Parity. Income growth in the Dynamic country is partially neutralized by high domestic inflation in its local services, forcing households to allocate a very significant portion of their family wage to cover essential services. Furthermore, these services are provided through an ecosystem that is structurally more costly than its European counterpart.

In parallel, the Social country benefits indirectly from globalized international trade flows. On the one hand, it imports technological goods produced at a lower relative cost in the Dynamic country and capitalizes on foreign productivity gains. On the other hand, its population continues to access affordable and efficient local services provided by its welfare model.

The final statistical outcome demonstrates that a country's broad technological advantage does not mechanically translate into a proportional improvement in the standard of living for the majority of its citizens. When measuring Real Consumption, the well-being gap between the two economies grows at a substantially slower rate than the purely technological differential, confirming that efficiency in the provision of local services acts as a powerful and silent mechanism of economic compensation.

### Conclusion and synthesis of the debate

This theoretical exercise is particularly useful for reconciling two opposing views of the modern economic landscape.

On the one hand, the most critical diagnoses of European stagnation are accurate in pointing out the progressive loss of technological competitiveness. The endemic inability to lead in innovation or retain specialized talent against markets offering more attractive incomes represents a real and pressing strategic challenge.

On the other hand, the widespread narrative of continental decline is incomplete when the cost of living analysis in the most dynamic areas is omitted. By deflating consumption through Purchasing Power Parity, evidence shows that the average citizen in the Social country maintains a highly competitive standard of living, and even superior in certain dimensions of financial security.

The aggregate well-being of a modern economy does not depend exclusively on its ability to lead frontier technology sectors, but rests deeply on the efficiency with which it provides daily basic services. These services make up the vast majority of household expenditure and consumption. Ultimately, Europe's solid capacity to maintain high aggregate efficiency in its social model clearly explains why the quality of life for its citizens has not deteriorated to the dramatic extent that strict nominal production indicators would suggest.
