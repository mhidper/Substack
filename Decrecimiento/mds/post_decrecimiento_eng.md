# The economic limits of degrowth and the viability of absolute decoupling

## An analysis of the social, institutional, and technological costs of a planned contraction compared with the opportunities of the green transition

Economic growth is frequently analyzed as a mere quantitative indicator or a statistical abstraction. However, it represents one of the most effective instruments for the improvement of social welfare and the reduction of poverty. According to World Bank data, the proportion of the global population living below the extreme poverty line fell from over 35% in 1990 to approximately 8.5% prior to the pandemic in 2020. This decline represents more than one billion individuals escaping conditions of extreme economic vulnerability. Furthermore, this expansive dynamic has been consistently associated with rising life expectancy, reductions in infant mortality, and historically high literacy rates worldwide.

For developing economies in Asia, Africa, and Latin America, growth is not an academic debate regarding superfluous consumption, but rather the primary channel to secure basic nutrition, sanitation infrastructure, and education. Consequently, advocacy for degrowth, originating primarily in high-income Western societies, introduces complex ethical and economic dilemmas. Demanding material restrictions from regions that have yet to achieve a minimum threshold of welfare can be perceived as an imposition that perpetuates global divergence and restricts the legitimate right of these nations to develop.

Defined as a proposed planned and selective reduction of production and consumption in industrialized economies, degrowth seeks to mitigate pressures on the biosphere and contain climate change. Nevertheless, a rigorous economic evaluation of this strategy reveals significant structural friction. A deliberate contraction of productive activity induces effects analogous to a prolonged recession, which translates into persistent unemployment, contracting household incomes, and declining tax revenues that compromise the funding of essential public services. The premise that a shrinking output can be equitably distributed underestimates the political and social tensions associated with dividing a contracting resource base.

From an institutional and social cohesion perspective, the viability of a planned contraction within a democratic framework remains highly improbable. Centralized coordination of sectoral reductions would require levels of state intervention incompatible with traditional market and individual freedoms. Conversely, if contraction were to occur in an unplanned manner, the instability of value chains and financial uncertainty would threaten basic economic order. Historical evidence indicates that prolonged periods of economic contraction erode social consensus and foster polarization. In an environment where aggregate wealth diminishes, competition for resources intensifies, which frequently encourages populist discourse and challenges the legitimacy of democratic institutions.

In addition, the implementation of rationing or directed resource allocation systems presents insurmountable operational difficulties without the recourse to coercive measures. Defining objective criteria for what constitutes a sufficient level of consumption generates power asymmetries between planning regulators and citizens. Historically, such bureaucratic structures have encouraged the emergence of informal markets, inefficient arbitrage, and institutional distrust, thereby weakening social capital.

Another critical limit of degrowth concerns the driver of the ecological transition, namely technological innovation. The development and deployment of green technologies require massive investments in research and development (R&D) from both public and private sources. A scenario of economic contraction and depressed markets reduces financial incentives and the self-financing capacity of firms to undertake long-term projects. Consequently, a slowdown in economic activity could deprive society of the technical tools necessary to decarbonize the productive matrix.

In contrast to the risks of contraction, the strategy of sustainable or green growth offers a viable alternative through absolute decoupling, which occurs when gross domestic product grows while resource consumption and environmental emissions decline. Developed countries within the OECD have demonstrated the empirical viability of this mechanism by reducing aggregate greenhouse gas emissions while maintaining positive rates of economic growth. This trend has been driven by cost reductions in renewable generation, with unit costs decreasing by more than 80% over the last decade, as well as advancements in precision agriculture and biotechnology.

Consolidating this trajectory requires rigorous public policies. The adoption of carbon pricing, the elimination of fossil fuel subsidies, and the funding of basic research are indispensable to correct environmental externalities and channel private capital toward sustainability. In conclusion, addressing the ecological crisis does not require a programmatic rejection of growth, but rather its technological and regulatory reorientation, which reconciles human welfare and ecosystem stability through pragmatic optimism and innovation.
